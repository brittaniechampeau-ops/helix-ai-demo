CASE STUDY 1: OHIO MANUFACTURING CO. (SILVERPOINT CAPITAL)
Industry: Industrial Manufacturing | Deal size: $47M EV | Completed: 2024

Situation:
Silverpoint Capital was evaluating the acquisition of a precision manufacturing company in Ohio. The management team presented well — a CFO with a Big 4 background and a COO who had previously worked at a publicly traded industrial firm. Standard diligence was proceeding normally.

What Webb Advisory Found:
During behavioral interviews, we identified that the COO operated in a fully consensus-driven decision-making style. Every significant decision required full buy-in from all direct reports before action was taken. While this had created a stable culture during the company's organic growth phase, it was a significant transition risk for a PE-backed environment where speed and accountability would be expected post-close.

Document review corroborated: board minutes showed long deliberation cycles and several deferred strategic decisions. Two former employees referenced in our calls independently flagged the same pattern.

Action Taken:
Silverpoint did not walk away — the core business was sound. Instead, they restructured the management incentive package to separate individual accountability from team consensus, and embedded an operating partner in the business from day one with a clear mandate to model a faster decision cadence.

Outcome:
Meridian reported it as one of their cleanest first-year integrations in the past five years. The COO adapted well once individual accountability was made explicit. No management team changes were required in the first 18 months post-close.

CASE STUDY 2: REGIONAL STAFFING FIRM (CASTLEGATE EQUITY)
Industry: Professional Staffing | Deal size: $28M EV | Completed: 2023

Situation:
Castlegate was evaluating a regional professional staffing business. Revenue had grown 35% over three years. The founder-CEO wanted to stay on post-close. Castlegate's concern: could he operate effectively with a board above him after 12 years as sole decision-maker?

What Webb Advisory Found:
The CEO was highly capable operationally but showed clear autonomy bias in interviews — he had no existing reporting structures, had never presented to a board, and described previous investor interest as something he'd "passed on to maintain flexibility." Reference calls confirmed he was effective but ran the business as a sole principal in all meaningful ways.

We also surfaced a succession risk: the #2 leader (VP Operations) had been actively recruited twice in the past 18 months and had declined primarily due to loyalty to the founder, not compensation. That loyalty dynamic would likely shift post-close.

Action Taken:
Castlegate redesigned the CEO retention package with a structured performance scorecard tied to board-defined metrics — giving him clear success criteria rather than leaving the relationship ambiguous. They also accelerated the VP Operations' equity grant timeline to retain her through the transition.

Outcome:
Both leaders are still with the business 2.5 years post-close. Revenue is up 18% in the first full year under PE ownership. CEO reported in a check-in call that the board structure was "harder than expected but worth it."

CASE STUDY 3: SOFTWARE COMPANY — MANAGEMENT RISK IDENTIFIED (FUND: UNDISCLOSED)
Industry: B2B SaaS | Deal size: $85M EV | Completed: 2025

Situation:
A growth equity fund (name withheld per NDA) was evaluating a B2B SaaS company with strong ARR growth. The CEO was founder-led, early 40s, had previously exited one business. Initial impression: experienced, strategic, low-risk.

What Webb Advisory Found:
During interviews, we noticed inconsistencies between the CEO's described product roadmap and the actual product development timeline in documents. When probed, responses became vague and deflective. Reference calls with two former customers (obtained through our network independently of management-provided references) surfaced concerns about the CEO's transparency during a prior product delay.

We also identified that the VP Product, who was critical to the roadmap the fund was underwriting, had not been included in any retention conversation and was visibly disengaged in the interview we conducted with her.

Our assessment: Elevated execution risk. The CEO's pattern suggested information management rather than information sharing under pressure. The VP Product departure risk was high.

Action Taken:
The fund used our report to open a direct conversation with the CEO about the inconsistencies. He acknowledged one documentation gap but was defensive about the broader pattern. The fund renegotiated the deal structure to include a 30% earnout tied to product milestones and added contractual requirements for quarterly board updates with defined reporting templates.

The VP Product was put on a retention package with an accelerated grant.

Outcome:
Deal closed. VP Product is still on board. 12 months post-close, the CEO has adapted to the reporting cadence. One product milestone was missed; the earnout structure meant the fund retained leverage to address it without a contentious renegotiation.

CASE STUDY 4: FAMILY-OWNED DISTRIBUTION BUSINESS (MERIDIAN PARTNERS)
Industry: Industrial Distribution | Deal size: $31M EV | Completed: 2023

Situation:
Meridian was evaluating a family-owned industrial distribution business. The founder was retiring and had named his son as CEO two years prior. Meridian needed to assess whether the son was ready to lead a PE-backed scaling effort.

What Webb Advisory Found:
The son (CEO) was commercially talented — strong client relationships, good market instincts. However, he had never built a management team from scratch. The current team was almost entirely inherited from his father's era. He had not had a hard conversation with any of them, and one regional manager (who accounted for 28% of revenue) had been underperforming for 18 months without formal feedback.

We flagged this as a leadership maturity gap, not a character flaw — but a real operational risk.

Action Taken:
Meridian required as a close condition that the business hire an experienced COO within 60 days post-close, with the COO reporting to the new CEO. Meridian identified the candidate through their operating partner network.

Outcome:
The COO hire was completed within 45 days. The underperforming regional manager was put on a formal performance plan and subsequently separated from the business at month 8. The CEO has described the COO relationship as "the most important thing Meridian did for me." Revenue was flat in year one but rebounded with a new regional manager in place.
